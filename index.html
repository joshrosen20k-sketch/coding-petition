A few weeks ago, my mom and I were talking about how she uses AI to help her through her job. That immediately caught my interest — within seconds I had so many ideas just waiting to be built. A few days later it started. My mom and I worked for a couple of hours every night, and slowly but surely I finished my very first project: editing my Google Calendar to make scheduling easier. Adding AI courses to District 109 schools could benefit hundreds of kids — teaching them to use AI as a tool for their passions, not a shortcut. But giving kids access to AI requires real precautions. Schools should use education-focused AI tools that guide students rather than do the work for them, and students should be taught how to ask questions that actually move them forward. In the past, when AI was handed to me for studying, I had no idea how to use it properly. You and the district office have the opportunity to change that — to build something that helps students like me grow into this technology the right way.

Why This Matters

Coding and AI are no longer just for tech companies — they are becoming part of everyday life. From medicine to music, sports to science, understanding these tools is quickly becoming as important as reading and math. Students who learn them now will have a major advantage in whatever career they choose.

"The best way to predict the future is to invent it." — Alan Kay, computer scientist

What We're Asking For

We're asking our school to introduce dedicated Coding and AI courses — not just as an elective, but as a core part of our education. These courses would teach students real skills: how to build things, how to think logically, and how AI tools actually work.

"Education is the most powerful weapon which you can use to change the world." — Nelson Mandela
